# yoniso manasikāra / ayoniso manasikāra

## Danish rendering:
Grundigt fokus / overfladisk fokus (“thorough focus / superficial focus”).

## Translations consulted:
- Sujato: Rational application of mind and irrational application of mind.
- Bodhi: Wise attention and unwise attention.
- Horner: Wise attention and unwise attention.
- Lie (Norwegian): “Man kan bruke tankene fornuftig og man kan bruke dem ufornuftig.”
- Suddhāso: Application of mind based on an understanding of causality, and application of mind not based on an understanding of causality.
- Anālayo: “Attention purposely directed, in a manner that is wise, thorough, and appropriate.”

## Definition
**Yoniso manasikāra** denotes a specific way of directing the mind toward a phenomenon, where the focus is **thorough**, i.e. **complete and not stopping at the surface**. Its opposite, **ayoniso manasikāra**, denotes a **superficial** focus, which takes a phenomenon's immediate appearance at face value and sustains habitual perception and reactivity.

This definition is deliberately more restrained than earlier drafts of this note (see “Translation problem” below for the full process). The word *yoniso* occurs across quite different contexts — the use of cloth (Kd21), a manner of questioning (AN 3.5), reflection on food (the moderation-in-eating formula), besides *manasikāra* — and a translation meant to cover all of these should not rest on a narrow, doctrinally motivated meaning that only holds in some of them.

## Etymology
##### yoni
Pāli/Sanskrit *yoni* means “womb, source, origin, matrix.” In Vedic poetry the expression *yonāv ṛtasya* (“the womb of truth”) occurs, illustrating the use of *yoni* as a metaphor for the cosmic source of order and truth. This etymology is historically interesting, and is the background for why several translators (Sujato among them, as well as earlier drafts of this note) have proposed a causal, “to-the-source” meaning for *yoniso*. **This inference is, however, contested** (see below) and should not be given decisive weight in the translation of the word itself — it is mentioned only as a possible, not a settled, semantic background.

##### manasikāra
Derived from *manasi-kṛ* (“to make in the mind,” “to bring into the mind”). In the Abhidhamma, *manasikāra* is classified as a universal mental factor that **directs the mind toward an object**, establishing the cognitive contact that makes further processing possible. In modern Danish, **fokus** (“focus”) captures this directedness more precisely than “attention,” which in this translation is reserved for *sati*.

##### The compound
**Yoniso manasikāra = focus directed thoroughly at a phenomenon, without stopping at its superficial appearance.**

## Semantic and doctrinal function
##### Epistemological function
Yoniso manasikāra is the mental operation that makes it possible to see phenomena **thoroughly**, i.e. without being stopped by their immediate, surface appearance. In contexts where a sutta describes the consequences of yoniso/ayoniso manasikāra in terms of arising and cessation (e.g. MN 2, SN 46.2), this thoroughness shows up in practice as an ability to see the conditioned nature of phenomena — but this is a **doctrinal consequence of thoroughness in that specific context**, not a claim that the word *yoniso* itself means “causal” or “cause-directed” in general (see the extended discussion below).

##### Meditative function
In meditation practice, yoniso manasikāra functions as the factor that **redirects focus** from reactivity and habitual perception toward a thorough investigation of a phenomenon's nature. In passages such as MN 2 and SN 46.2, it is precisely this thoroughness that makes it possible to see arising and cessation, conditions and dependencies.

##### Ethical-psychological function
In the suttas, yoniso manasikāra is a **necessary condition** for overcoming the five hindrances, for developing the seven awakening factors, and for transforming ignorance into insight. Ayoniso manasikāra, conversely, is a **fundamental cause** of ignorance, clinging, and suffering.

## Textual context
In **MN 2**, yoniso manasikāra functions as the central mechanism that allows the practitioner to identify, analyse, and dissolve the mental hindrances. The sutta presents it as the practical key to transforming the mind's reactions and establishing a stable, thorough focus.

In **MN 1**, yoniso manasikāra is implicit in the way the awakened one sees phenomena “as they are.” Here the concept functions as an underlying epistemic structure.

In the **Abhidhamma**, manasikāra is classified as a universal mental factor, but *yoniso* adds a qualitative dimension that makes the factor either insight-generating or misleading.

## Translation problem

##### The problem with “wise attention”
“Wise” is semantically too broad and too moralising — it doesn't specify *what* the wisdom consists in, merely pushing the question one step back. “Attention” is, moreover, reserved for **sati** in this translation, making it terminologically imprecise to use the same word for *manasikāra*. “Focus” was chosen as the running rendering of *manasikāra* because it matches the core meaning (directedness toward an object) without colliding with *sati*, and because it is not already occupied by other closely related Pāli terms (e.g. *paṭisaṅkhā*, “to consider,” which can also take *yoniso* as an adverb, though it is not part of this sutta's formula).

##### The path to “thorough/superficial”
This note went through an extended clarification process in connection with the translation of MN 2, in dialogue with several users on the SuttaCentral forum (July 2026). That process is reproduced here because it grounds the final choice:

1. **Starting point:** “Causally-directed focus,” grounded in the etymology of *yoni* (“source, origin”) and in the fact that MN 2 and related texts (e.g. SN 46.2) describe yoniso/ayoniso manasikāra in clearly causal terms (the arising/ceasing of āsavas, the growth/decline of hindrances).
2. **One user (Pasanna) pointed to a counter-example from Kd21** (Vinaya), showing *yoniso* used of monks making full use of cloth scraps (“nothing wasted”) — a concrete, non-mental usage pointing to a broader meaning: “completely/thoroughly,” rather than a specifically causal reference.
3. **One user (Dogen)** proposed that yoniso manasikāra is self-validating: if the conclusion is wrong, one was by definition not being yoniso — which could be reconciled with the causal reading by taking a conditioned phenomenon's “bottom” to simply be its conditions.
4. **A decisive methodological counterpoint (Sunyo, citing Anālayo):** that practice works a certain way (e.g. that overcoming the hindrances requires understanding their causes) proves nothing about what the *word* yoniso itself means. Basing a word's meaning on practice rather than linguistic analysis is a slippery slope, highly prone to confirmation bias. In support of this, the following were highlighted:
   - AN 3.5 / DN 4: *yoniso pañhaṃ pucchati* (“to ask a question *yoniso*”) — concerns a well-formed, relevant question, not obviously causality.
   - A formulaic kinship between *yoniso manasikāra* (SN 12.10), *sādhukaṃ manasikāra* (“thoroughly/carefully,” Ud 1.1), and the combined *yoniso sādhukaṃ manasikāra* (SN 12.62), suggesting that *yoniso* functions as a near-synonym of *sādhukaṃ*.
   - **The moderation-in-eating formula**: a monk reflects *yoniso* on his food — not for fun or adornment, but only to sustain the body. This is a reflection on **purpose and motivation**, not a tracing of causal chains, and an attempt to read causality into it seems forced.
   - **Anālayo's definition**, adopted as the most cautious and philologically well-grounded: “attention purposely directed, in a manner that is wise, thorough, and appropriate” — without a causal component.
   - The parallel to **sammā** (“right”): that right practice always involves causality does not prove that the word *sammā* itself means “causal” — the same argument applies to *yoniso*.
5. **Conclusion:** The causal reading was abandoned as a general translation of *yoniso*, but retained as a **contextual, interpretive remark** for the specific passages (MN 2, SN 46.2) where thoroughness, in practice, shows up as the ability to trace phenomena to their conditions — without claiming this to be the word's meaning in general.

##### Why “thorough” rather than “to-the-bottom” or “proper”
“Thorough” (Danish: *grundigt*) was preferred for several reasons: it renders Anālayo's own word directly; it sits close to *sādhukaṃ* (“thoroughly/carefully”), the nearest documented synonym relation; it forms a more idiomatic Danish antonym pair with “superficial” than alternatives such as “to-the-bottom/superficial” or “proper/improper”; and, as a conventionalised, “dead” image (Danish *grund* = ground/foundation), it carries a faint, unasserted resonance with both the Kd21 image and the earlier causal discussion, without turning that resonance into a claim about the word's meaning.

## Short definition for use in running text
> *Yoniso manasikāra* denotes a way of directing the mind toward phenomena where the focus is thorough and does not stop at the surface. In contexts such as MN 2, this thoroughness shows up as the ability to trace phenomena to their conditions — but this is a contextual consequence, not the word's general meaning.

## Comment
Sujato writes: “Rational application of mind” (*yoniso manasikāra*) is a distinctively Buddhist term. It draws on the frequent Vedic image of the “womb of truth” (*yonāv ṛtasya*, Rig Veda 9.13.9), the source of the laws and patterns that govern the natural order. The idea is that by applying the mind by way of cause or reason the hidden truth can be uncovered. Here the meditator's insight is framed not as uncovering the objective truth about the world, but as reflexively understanding the means of insight itself.

This reading was the starting point for the initial work on the concept, but has since been considerably nuanced through the discussion referenced above under “Translation problem.” The etymological connection to *yoni* as “source” remains interesting background knowledge, but should not in itself carry the weight of the translation choice.
